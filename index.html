Analysis Overview

The core challenge of cartography lies in the impossible task of flattening a 3D sphere onto a 2D surface without compromising geographic truth. This analysis examines how different map projections—Robinson, Goode Homolosine, Mercator, and Bonne—manipulate area, direction, and distance to serve specific purposes. By comparing these mathematical frameworks, I illustrated how a choice in projection can unintentionally bias a viewer's perception of continental size or navigational accuracy. The resulting map serves as a visual argument for why "accuracy" is relative to the map’s intended use. Ultimately, this project highlights the critical need for GIS analysts to select Coordinate Reference Systems that preserve the integrity of their specific data.

  • Robinson: A natural-looking compromise; curved poles.
  • Goode Homolosine: Interrupted oceans; accurate land proportions.
  • Mercator: Navigational angles preserved; polar size inflation.
  • Bonne: Heart-shaped; distortion increases from center.
Skills Learned
  • Map Projections & CRS: Mastered coordinate systems and mathematical impacts.
  • Defining vs. Projecting: Technical distinction between defining and converting CRS.
  • ArcGIS Pro Proficiency: Advanced layout and layer management.
Analysis Overview

Urban food deserts present a complex challenge for city planners seeking to improve community health through equitable retail access. In this study of Philadelphia, I performed a multi-criteria vector analysis to identify optimal sites for new healthy food retailers that meet strict policy and economic requirements. The process involved synthesizing disparate datasets—including transit proximity, economic empowerment zones, and existing market competitors—to isolate viable parcels of land. By applying geometric operations like buffers and erasures, I filtered out saturated markets while prioritizing areas within reach of public transportation. This spatial narrative reveals the narrow "sweet spots" where investment meets community need. The final output provides a data-driven roadmap for tackling food insecurity through strategic urban intervention.

  • Investment: Within Empowerment Zones.
  • Transit: Within 2,000ft of rail stations.
  • Market Gap: 1,200ft buffer from existing stores.
Skills Learned
  • Vector Analysis: Buffer, Dissolve, Union, Intersect, and Erase workflows.
  • Advanced Spatial Joins: Synthesizing transit and socioeconomic datasets.
  • Cartographic Labeling: Using dynamic expressions for interest areas.
Analysis Overview

Raw demographic tables often hide the vibrant, localized stories of migration and settlement that define a nation’s identity. Using 2010 U.S. Census data, I transformed millions of data points into a visual narrative of the Hispanic population’s distribution across the contiguous United States. By employing dot density and choropleth mapping techniques, the analysis moves beyond simple counts to reveal how geography and history shape community concentrations. The map highlights massive regional clusters in the Southwest and Florida, but also uncovers smaller, significant "island" communities along major metropolitan corridors. This visualization effectively demonstrates how spatial patterns of settlement are influenced by proximity to borders and urban labor markets. It serves as a powerful tool for understanding the demographic shifts that continue to redefine the American landscape.

Skills Learned
  • Thematic Mapping: Proficient in designing Dot Density and Choropleth maps to visualize population distribution.
  • Census Data Integration: Applied knowledge of Census Bureau datasets within a geospatial context to analyze demographic trends.
  • Dynamic Map & Layout Linking: Mastered the relationship between map views and layout elements, ensuring legends and scales update dynamically.
