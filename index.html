Fundamentals of GIS Final Project

Analysis Overview

This project explores how map projections influence our perception of geographic "truth." Because a 3D sphere cannot be perfectly flattened, every projection inherently distorts area, direction, or distance. As an analyst, choosing the correct projection is critical to ensuring data-driven conclusions are accurate.

  • Robinson: A compromise projection that distorts everything slightly to create a visually "natural" look, representing poles as lines rather than points.
  • Goode Homolosine: Minimizes land distortion by "interrupting" the oceans, maintaining accurate proportions at the expense of map continuity.
  • Mercator: Preserves angles for navigation but severely inflates the size of regions near the poles, making Greenland and Alaska appear disproportionately large.
  • Bonne: A pseudoconic projection that creates a heart-shaped map; distortion increases significantly as you move away from the central meridian.
Skills Learned
  • Map Projections & CRS: Mastered coordinate systems and understood the visual and mathematical impact of different global projections.
  • Defining vs. Projecting: Learned the technical distinction between defining a missing projection and converting (re-projecting) an existing CRS.
  • ArcGIS Pro Proficiency: Advanced technical skills in layout creation, layer customization, and multi-layer data management.
Analysis Overview

This analysis identifies optimal locations for new healthy food retailers in Philadelphia. The workflow utilizes multi-criteria decision-making to find areas that maximize community impact and accessibility while avoiding market saturation.

Spatial Criteria Applied:

  • Investment: Must be within a Philadelphia Empowerment Zone.
  • Transit Access: Within 2,000 feet of a subway or regional rail station.
  • Market Gap: Not within 1,200 feet of existing farmers markets or Healthy Corner Stores.
  • Scale: Prioritized larger contiguous areas to facilitate property acquisition.
Skills Learned
  • Vector Analysis Operations: Executed complex spatial workflows using Buffer, Dissolve, Union, Intersect, and Erase tools to isolate suitable parcels.
  • Advanced Spatial Joins: Synthesized disparate datasets—including transit hubs and socioeconomic zones—to perform deeper geographic analysis.
  • Cartographic Labeling: Utilized dynamic labeling and expressions to effectively communicate and highlight specific areas of interest.
  • Map Design Excellence: Iterated on map layouts to improve clarity, symbology, and overall visual storytelling in ArcGIS.
Analysis Overview

This project visualizes the spatial distribution of the Hispanic population across the contiguous United States using 2010 U.S. Census data. By aggregating demographic data at the state level, this map highlights significant regional concentrations, particularly in the Southwest, Florida, and major metropolitan corridors.

The analysis demonstrates how thematic mapping—specifically the use of normalized color scales and dot density—can reveal patterns of migration and settlement that are often obscured in raw tabular data.

Skills Learned
  • Thematic Mapping Techniques: Proficient in designing Dot Density and Choropleth maps to visualize population distribution and density effectively.
  • Census Data Integration: Applied advanced knowledge of Census Bureau datasets within a geospatial context to analyze and present demographic trends.
  • Dynamic Map & Layout Linking: Mastered the relationship between map views and layout elements, ensuring legends and scales update dynamically to maintain cartographic integrity.
  • Multi-Layer Data Management: Organized and filtered complex geographic data layers to produce clear, actionable insights for demographic analysis.
